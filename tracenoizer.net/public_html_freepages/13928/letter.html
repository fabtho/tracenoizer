The runabouts are basically souped-up golf carts, equipped with enough battery power to go as fast as 25 miles per hour - nearly twice as fast as traditional golf carts - and to cover a 30-mile range. Why is horsepower-happy Detroit so keen on rolling out these low-power contraptions. Because NEVs are the cheapest way for the automakers to comply with the letter of a California clean-air rule set to take effect in 2003 despite years of industry lobbying to kill it. The rule will require all but the smallest auto companies to sell thousands of "zero emission" vehicles, or else face caps on how many gasoline-powered cars and trucks, including cash-cow SUVs, they are allowed to sell in the state. In 2003 alone, as many as 30,900 NEVs could hit the pavement in the Golden State, according to a state estimate - despite questions about the vehicles' safety and whether they will provide much environmental benefit.
