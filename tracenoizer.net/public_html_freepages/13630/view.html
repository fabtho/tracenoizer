Wilber takes up Arthur Koestler's notion of the holon which is both a whole and a part. It leads to the notion of holarchy, the nested hierarchy of life, consciousness and meaning. Through a brilliant analysis of a variety of individuals and traditions of enquiry, Wilber argues that Western culture mistakenly assumed that rationality was the culmination and the end of evolution - whereas in this view it is simply a stage which may be transcended (and included) in a more integral view. The world of modernity, of industrialism, was constructed on a pattern that extinguished vertical distinctions and reduced them to the rationalistic interlocking elements of what he calls 'flatland'. Individuals and cultures were stripped of inner meaning and the external world (including the global ecology) was rendered into a set of things, mere resources.
Clarke identifies certain features of information production that are similar to the features of other types of commodity production such as a division of labour, a technological scheme which includes a skilled and disciplined workforce and a highly bureaucratized organizational scheme, and a raw material which is transformed through the course of production into a finished product, the information commodity. A political economy approach to news production rests on an understanding of a relationship between the media and the state. Michael Tracy, a British theorist, puts forward two views of this relationship, the liberal democratic view and the Marxist view. The liberal democratic view holds that the political process is a system that has several discrete parts. The media form one autonomous part as do other institutions such as the educational system, the judiciary, the health care system, etcetera.
