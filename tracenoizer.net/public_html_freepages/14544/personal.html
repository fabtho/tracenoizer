Part of my course work involved advanced exegesis, greek language studies and a great deal of other things. It was a fantastic experience for three reasons, One, some of the people I met today are among my closest friends to this day. Two, I had a teacher named Doug Farrow (who set insanely high academic standards) take personal interest in me and mentor me. He now teaches at McGill as Professor of Theology and I am deeply indebted to him to this day for his care, rigor and charisma. My goal is to use the questions as a platform for him to open up and share a measure of his life, values and experiences with us so that we can better understand who he is.

The benchmarks that follow are aimed at getting students to understand human social behavior, not at fostering social action or socializing future citizens. In everyday life, scientific knowledge is seldom essential or sufficient for guiding social action. Nevertheless, if scientific knowledge of how human society works is widely shared, it can contribute to better personal and public decision making. Exploring the findings of social science requires teachers and students to be both the observers and the observed. It takes time for students to learn how to shift roles.
