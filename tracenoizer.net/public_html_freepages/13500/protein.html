Superstructures Protein superstructures at exocytotic sites Thorsten Lang, Dietmar Riedel It is a major advantage of flat lawns of plasma membranes that the distribution of membrane proteins and membrane- associated proteins can be analyzed by fluorescence microscopy without any interference by labeled structures above or below the focal plane. Thus, unprecedented resolution of the distribution of proteins in the plane of the membrane can be achieved that is only limited by the resolutional limit of the light microscope. To our surprise we found that SNAREs and, in addition, other membrane constituents are not evenly distributed across the membrane surface but rather clustered in hot spots of high local protein density.
Likewise, if crystallization problems exist, MS can be a nice tool to identify possible sources of the problem. MS can also be used to detect and possibly identify impurities in crystallization reagents. In the case of recombinant protein samples, an accurate measurement of the molecular weight will inform one about the presence of post-translational modifications. Dependent on the physical properties of the compound and the MS technique used, the molecular weight may be determined within an accuracy of one Dalton. The use of MS measurements will give sequence information where classical methods fail or may lead to ambiguous results as in the case of N-terminal blocked peptides, glycosylation or identification of the C-terminus.
