. This time the settlers really wanted to defeat and punish the Sioux. Brown and his men walked over the road to Redwood Ferry and eventually to Birch Coulee, where the battle would take place. They picked up and buried the bodies of settlers they found along the way. Early the next morning, they divided to look for more bodies, eventually finding a total of forty-five white victims that day.
Despite frequent failure of the government to provide aid promised for the transition, many Dakotas made successes of their farms. 500 whites lost their lives in the conflict; the Dakota dead were never counted. For their part in the conflict, 38 Dakota men were hanged in the largest mass execution in American history. Many Dakotas fled to Canada during and after the conflict, remembering British promises to protect them after Dakotas aided them in their fight against the French years before. All other Dakotas found themselves exiled from Minnesota, their homeland for hundreds of years.
This captivating and heart wrenching book concludes with a final scene taking place shortly after the massacre at Wounded Knee. Dead and injured Indians are being laid to rest along the straw covered floor of an Episcopalian church. A banner over the pulpit reads, PEACE ON EARTH, GOOD WILL TO MEN. ..
It was at Fort Ridgely thirteen miles across the Minnesota River that Captain Marsh received news of the attack on the Lower Agency. With a meager count of forty-six soldiers, Captain Marsh departed for the Lower Agency. Upon reaching Redwood Ferry, Marsh and his men were encouraged by White Dog, an Indian across the river, to cross via an available ferry boat to hold council. The unsuspecting troops made for the boat when they were ambushed by a fleet of Dakota warriors. In a state of panic, Marsh attempted to lead his men across the river to escape.
