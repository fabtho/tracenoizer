In many cases the way in which cultural policy is dealt with is clearly determined on response to specific demographical characteristics. Switzerland is not alone, about forty million people living within the former European Community speak an indigenous language other than the majority language of the state within which they are living. As a result of immigration the situation is further complicated, even in a low population country such as Sweden as many as 140 languages were estimated to be spoken by different families. There is no doubt that within cultural policy making, language policy is taking a leading role in many European regions and this is reflected in other responses to the questionnaire, notably from Spain and Italy. The situation for languages is exasperated in countries or regions where the majority language has a minority status beyond its borders.
The Croatian and Canadian replies bring in further dimensions to our analyses. The Croatian central government budget for international cultural co-operation, 1. 5 million Euro, reflects the situation in most of the new democracies(6) . Canada also offers a different pattern with rather low budget figures, but considerable allocations for co-productions. These two cases suggest that low government budget outlays do not always mean stagnation, and high do not inevitably imply high level of activity.
