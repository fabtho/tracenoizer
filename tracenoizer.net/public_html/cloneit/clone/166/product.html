Eco-design involves far more than creating a product that is easy to dismantle and recycle at the end of its lifetime, experts say. Successful eco-design also demands commitment from top management, and its principles must be integrated as a strategy from the birth of an idea through the design, manufacturing, marketing and entire life cycle of a product. Tischner, eco-design means developing a product that is ecologically, economically and socially sustainable.
To do this we should make sure that the use of consumer goods is divided among the maximum number of people, in order to reduce the impact on the environment as much as we can. The principle of eco-efficiency should apply to the entire life cycle of goods, from the excavation of raw materials, through production and use to recycling and ultimate disposal. This requires the designer to consider the original purpose of the product, and how this service could be fulfilled with a minimum of harmful environmental effects during the product's entire 'cradle-to-the-grave' life cycle. Such systematic solutions are usually more effective than changes to the product. But, since they require behavioural changes on the part of producers and consumers, they may be more difficult to implement.
