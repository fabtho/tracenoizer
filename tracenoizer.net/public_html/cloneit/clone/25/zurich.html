Garden expositions also left behind lasting traces and monuments, as seen in parts of Zurichs lakeside promenade, or the Bruglinger Area in Basel. The fact that landscape architecture has become the focus of such expositions is by and large a phenomenon of the 20th century. The turning point was in 1959 when Zurich hosted the G59 (Horticulture Exposition 1959), the first garden exposition with an extensive and explicit cultural focus, and one that formulated an independent approach to garden design. Highlight of this new understanding was the ''Garden of the Poets'' designed by Ernst Cramer. This research project is primarily dedicated to securing sources, documenting and analyzing the history of Swiss garden expositions.
